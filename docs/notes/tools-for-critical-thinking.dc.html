PART I — SEEING CAUSE & EVIDENCE
01
Causal inference without an experiment
You can draw valid causal conclusions from data you merely observed.
+
SCOTT CUNNINGHAM · CAUSAL INFERENCE: THE MIXTAPE · 2021

The first rule of any stats class is that you can't prove causation without a controlled experiment. Using causal diagrams and natural experiments, this book shows that isn't quite true — and that's exactly what makes it so valuable.

THE HEURISTIC

Before you believe A caused B, stop and ask: is there some third thing causing both?

DIAGRAM — THE CONFOUNDER

Also: people who take vitamins live longer — not necessarily from the vitamins, but because the kind of person who takes them also exercises and sees doctors. Find the hidden variable and you stop being fooled by half the statistics you meet.

02
Probability is belief, not frequency
A precise measure of how strongly you should believe, given the evidence.
+
E. T. JAYNES · PROBABILITY THEORY: THE LOGIC OF SCIENCE · 2003

A probability isn't just a frequency — it's a measure of how strongly you should believe something given your evidence. As new evidence arrives, you update that belief in proportion to how strong the evidence actually is.

THE HEURISTIC

Always start from the base rate.

DIAGRAM — THE 99%-ACCURATE TEST

Test positive for a rare disease on a 99%-accurate test and your gut screams you have it. But if only 1 in 10,000 people is sick, the test still throws ~100 false alarms for every real case — so your actual chance is about 1%.

03
Information is surprise
A message informs only to the extent it reduces your uncertainty.
+
DAVID MacKAY · INFORMATION THEORY, INFERENCE, AND LEARNING ALGORITHMS · 2003

If something were completely predictable, learning it would tell you nothing at all. Information is measured by how much surprise it removes.

THE HEURISTIC

Feedback that only confirms what you expected has taught you nothing. Seek the surprising signal.

DIAGRAM — INFORMATION vs PREDICTABILITY

The meeting where everyone nodded, the friend who agrees with everything, the test question you already knew cold — all carry zero information. The result that didn't fit and the person who disagrees are where the actual information is hiding.

PART II — THE LIMITS OF MODELS
04
Overfitting
Explain every past twist perfectly and you've fit the noise — then fail on new data.
+
HASTIE, TIBSHIRANI & FRIEDMAN · THE ELEMENTS OF STATISTICAL LEARNING · 2009

Build a model complex enough to explain every twist in your past data and it ends up fitting the random noise — then fails completely the moment it meets new data. Real prediction is generalization.

THE HEURISTIC

Be suspicious of any story that explains every past event flawlessly.

DIAGRAM — FIT THE NOISE vs FIT THE TREND

The market analyst with a confident, airtight reason for every single move last year almost certainly can't predict next year. Having more detailed explanation can hurt your predictions more than it helps.

05
Markov chains — memorylessness
In many systems the next state depends only on the current state, not the whole path.
+
J. R. NORRIS · MARKOV CHAINS · 1997

A strange, freeing property: in a huge range of systems the system has no memory of its own path. Your phone finishing your sentence is mostly guessing the next word from the one you just typed — not the whole paragraph behind it.

THE HEURISTIC

Before predicting what something will do next, ask how much of its past it actually depends on.

DIAGRAM — THE NEXT STEP FORGETS THE PATH

The catch: this isn't true of all human behavior. A person carries their entire history with them, and treating them as memoryless will burn you. The real skill is telling which kind of system you're looking at — independent, dependent, or dynamical.

06
Chaos — deterministic ≠ predictable
Two nearly identical starting points can spiral into wildly different outcomes.
+
STEVEN STROGATZ · NONLINEAR DYNAMICS AND CHAOS · 1994

The butterfly effect with full mathematical rigor: in nonlinear systems, tiny differences at the start explode into completely different endings — even when the system follows fixed, deterministic rules.

THE HEURISTIC

Hold your long-range forecasts loosely — about markets, careers, anything complex.

DIAGRAM — SENSITIVE DEPENDENCE

Tiny, unmeasurable differences today can completely dominate where things end up — in your life or in the lives of others. Mature humility, not false precision.

PART III — HANDLING UNCERTAINTY & HIDDEN STRUCTURE
07
Monte Carlo — don't solve it, simulate it
When a problem is too complex for a clean formula, run thousands of random trials.
+
ROBERT & CASELLA · MONTE CARLO STATISTICAL METHODS · 2004

Generate thousands of random trials, let them play out, and read the answer straight off the results. It's how weather forecasting tames the butterfly effect — running the model hundreds of times with tiny variations, then reporting the spread. That's where "70% chance of rain" comes from.

THE HEURISTIC

Stop hunting for the single most likely outcome. Run the scenario many times and plan around the full range.

DIAGRAM — THE SPREAD OF SIMULATED OUTCOMES

Facing an uncertain decision, run it many times in your head — the good outcomes, the bad, the genuine disasters — and plan around the whole range, not the one future you happened to predict first.

08
Factor analysis — the latent variable
A few hidden factors secretly drive a large number of surface behaviors.
+
RICHARD GORSUCH · FACTOR ANALYSIS · 1983

A standard work on uncovering latent variables — the small number of hidden factors beneath a large number of observed behaviors.

THE HEURISTIC

When behaviors always seem to happen together, stop treating them as separate — look for the single factor beneath them.

DIAGRAM — ONE FACTOR, MANY BEHAVIORS

A coworker who is punctual, keeps a spotless desk, and plans everything isn't showing you three traits — they're showing you one. Find that latent variable and a confusing pile of details collapses into a single thing you can understand.

09
Latent classes — a category is not a verdict
Sorts people into hidden types — and carries the most important warning on the list.
+
COLLINS & LANZA · LATENT CLASS AND LATENT TRANSITION ANALYSIS · 2010

Instead of continuous factors, it sorts people into hidden categories — distinct types the data reveals. Powerful, and worth handling with care.

THE HEURISTIC — ETHICAL AS MUCH AS STATISTICAL

A category earns its place for one reason: it's useful for prediction and parsimonious. That is completely separate from any claim about the worth, rights, or destiny of the people inside it.

DIAGRAM — HIDDEN CATEGORIES IN THE DATA

As Kant put it, you must treat people as ends in themselves, never merely as means — and here, never merely as data points.

PART IV — THE HUMAN CAVEAT
10
The average is nobody
The model you fit to a huge group often describes no actual individual within it.
+
FARRELL & LEWANDOWSKY · COMPUTATIONAL MODELING OF COGNITION AND BEHAVIOR · 2018

Average a thousand people's learning curves together and you get a smooth, gentle slope — even though not one of them learned that way. They each learned in sudden jumps and frustrating plateaus. This is the aggregation artifact, a close cousin of the ecological fallacy.

THE HEURISTIC

The average is nobody. Resist applying a group-level statistic to the specific person in front of you — or to yourself.

DIAGRAM — SMOOTH AVERAGE vs REAL LEARNERS

The population pattern and the individual reality are often two completely different things — and often need two completely different models to capture.

11
Know your distribution
The only book here that isn't hard — and the foundation for all the rest.
+
BARRON'S · AP STATISTICS · WHERE TO START

If you don't yet have the basic statistical foundation for everything above, start here. Most events cluster around an average and taper off symmetrically — a bell curve.

THE HEURISTIC

Once you know what distribution you're looking at, you can judge a likely claim from an outlandish one.

DIAGRAM — THE TAIL IS YOUR SIGNAL

When something lands far out in the tail, that's your signal: either something unusual is happening, or the claim someone's making probably isn't true.

12
No master equation for a human being
The book that pulls the rug out from under the other 11 — in the best way.
+
SHLOMO STERNBERG · DYNAMICAL SYSTEMS · 2010 · (the mathematician, not the psychologist)

A rigorous treatment of how complex systems evolve over time, whose deepest lesson is this: human behavior is not simple linear causation. There are no fixed laws of human behavior, no master equation — no matter what the hypey self-help books tell you.

THE HEURISTIC — THE ONE TO KEEP

Never reduce a person to a single cause or a tidy one-line explanation.

DIAGRAM — MANY FORCES, NO SINGLE CAUSE

When someone acts in a way you don't understand, resist the easy story — "he's just lazy," "she's just like that." Eleven of these books teach you to model reality; this last one teaches the humility to know exactly where those models stop.

Eleven books build the model.
The twelfth tells you where it stops.
